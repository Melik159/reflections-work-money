# Pierre Campergue — Economic Work, Money, and the Formalism of Transformations

This repository preserves the economic corpus of **Pierre Campergue**.

The main manuscript currently archived here is:

> **Pierre Campergue**, *Reflections on the Economic Power of Work and the Mechanical Power of Money*.

The purpose of this repository is archival, bibliographic, and scholarly. It was created to preserve Pierre Campergue’s work after his passing, to make the corpus easier to reference, and to allow readers to access, study, discuss, and critically examine his theoretical contribution.

## Purpose of the repository

This repository is dedicated to the preservation of Pierre Campergue’s economic writings.

It aims to:

- preserve the manuscript in a stable public location;
- maintain clear attribution to Pierre Campergue;
- provide bibliographic metadata;
- make the work easier to cite;
- support scholarly reading and criticism;
- prevent the corpus from being reduced to an inaccurate or oversimplified summary.

This repository does not claim institutional validation, peer review, or academic consensus. It preserves the work as an original intellectual contribution.

## Scope of the corpus

Pierre Campergue’s work develops an unconventional economic framework centered on:

- economic Work;
- Money;
- Cash;
- Product;
- labour;
- productivity;
- price formation;
- purchasing power;
- productive and non-productive activity;
- creation, distribution, and use;
- the distinction between accounting flows and real economic transformations.

The manuscript attempts to rebuild economic vocabulary from first principles by separating concepts that are often confused in ordinary economic language.

## Important clarification

Pierre Campergue’s work should not be reduced to the statement that “economics is thermodynamics.”

His project is more precise.

The manuscript uses the discipline of thermomechanical formalism as a methodological framework: precise definitions, explicit variables, coherent transformations, and rigorous distinctions between different types of economic operations.

The variables remain economic variables. The work does not simply import physical notions such as heat, temperature, or entropy into economics.

The central issue is semantic, analytical, and methodological: economic reasoning becomes confused when it fails to distinguish between different meanings of words such as money, cash, work, labour, productivity, value, price, creation, distribution, and use.

## Central distinctions

A major part of Pierre Campergue’s work consists in distinguishing concepts that are often treated as equivalent.

In particular, the corpus distinguishes:

### Cash and Money

**Cash** refers to the monetary support, accounting sign, or circulating medium.

**Money** refers to the economic value or purchasing power carried by that support.

In this framework, creating or moving monetary units is not automatically equivalent to creating real purchasing power.

### Labour and Economic Work

**Labour** refers to a task, effort, job, or activity to be performed.

**Economic Work** refers to productive transformation: the work involved in changing productive conditions, reducing costs, increasing productivity, or modifying the economic structure of production.

This distinction is central to the corpus. It prevents the word “work” from being used ambiguously to describe both a human activity and a transformation with economic effects.

### Creation, distribution, and use

The manuscript also distinguishes between:

- creation;
- distribution;
- use.

These are not equivalent operations.

Distribution can allocate or reallocate what already exists. Creation concerns the conditions under which economic value or purchasing power can emerge. Use concerns the consumption or operation of what has been produced.

This distinction is important because the corpus does not treat every monetary movement as a productive transformation.

## Productive and non-productive operations

Pierre Campergue’s framework separates operations that transform real economic conditions from operations that mainly move, classify, allocate, or redistribute existing units.

This distinction should not be read as a moral judgment on individuals or professions.

In this corpus:

- a productive operation is an operation that transforms economic conditions;
- a non-productive or redistributive operation may still be socially useful, but it does not necessarily create new purchasing power or new productive capacity in the strict sense used by the manuscript.

The distinction is analytical, not moral.

## What the manuscript is about

The manuscript studies economic phenomena through the lens of transformations.

It asks how economic value, purchasing power, and productive capacity emerge, move, change, or disappear.

It is concerned with the difference between:

- monetary signs and real value;
- movement and transformation;
- accounting allocation and productive change;
- nominal monetary growth and real purchasing power;
- work as activity and Work as economic transformation.

The manuscript proposes that a rigorous economic analysis requires precise definitions before any theory of value, money, productivity, or growth can be constructed.

## What the manuscript is not

This repository does not present Pierre Campergue’s manuscript as:

- a standard economics textbook;
- a peer-reviewed economic model;
- an institutional position;
- an established academic consensus;
- a simple analogy between thermodynamics and economics;
- an econophysics model importing physical variables directly into economics.

The work should be read as an original theoretical essay and as a formal attempt to clarify economic language.
